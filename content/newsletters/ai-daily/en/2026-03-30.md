## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY: What Is an "Agent Harness" and Why Does It Matter More Than the Model?

When people talk about AI coding agents, they focus on the model — Opus, Sonnet, GPT-5. But the **harness** is equally important. A harness is the scaffolding around the model: how it receives tasks, what tools it can call, how it retries on failure, how long it's allowed to run, and how it reports results back.

Anthropic's new engineering post shows that harness design — not model upgrades — was the key to pushing Claude further on frontend design and long-running software tasks. Think of it this way: even the best chef can't cook well with broken equipment. Two teams using the exact same model can get wildly different results based on their harness design: timeouts, tool permissions, retry logic, and context management all compound.

This is why "which model is best?" is increasingly the wrong question. "Which harness gets the most out of this model?" matters just as much.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY: The OpenClaw Protocol Moment

Forget the individual repos for a second and look at the pattern. In the span of a week, NVIDIA, Cloudflare, ByteDance, NEAR Protocol, and multiple indie teams all shipped their own OpenClaw implementations — in Rust, on Workers, in containers, optimized for privacy, optimized for speed, optimized for enterprise.

OpenClaw isn't a product anymore. It's becoming a *protocol* — the way "Unix" stopped being one operating system and became a family of compatible implementations. The original project defined the interface; now the ecosystem is competing on runtime characteristics. This is exactly how infrastructure standards emerge, and it's happening at AI speed.

The question isn't which fork wins. It's whether the OpenClaw API surface becomes the de facto standard for personal AI agents the way REST became the standard for web APIs. If it does, every one of these forks is a bet that paid off.
