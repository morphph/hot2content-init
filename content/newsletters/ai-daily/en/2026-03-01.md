## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY

**What Is Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)?**

With the Qwen 3.5 series dominating this week's releases, it's worth understanding the architecture behind them. A Mixture-of-Experts model doesn't use all its parameters for every input. Instead, a "router" network selects a small subset of specialized "expert" sub-networks to process each token. For example, Qwen 3.5-397B-A17B has 397 billion total parameters but only activates ~17 billion per token. This means you get near-large-model quality at a fraction of the compute cost. Think of it like a hospital: instead of every doctor examining every patient, a triage nurse routes you to the right specialist. MoE models are why open-source is closing the gap with proprietary models so fast — they're dramatically more efficient to run.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY

**Anthropic vs. The Pentagon: A Line in the Sand**

The biggest AI story this week isn't a model release — it's a company saying no. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei published a statement rejecting Pentagon contract language that would have permitted Claude's use in mass surveillance of Americans and fully autonomous weapons. The contract language, framed as a "final offer" by the Department of War, included legal escape clauses that would let safeguards be overridden at will.

Trump responded by ordering all federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's AI. The move puts Anthropic in an unprecedented position: a leading AI company choosing principle over a massive government contract. OpenAI subsequently stated it shares Anthropic's "red lines" on military AI use, citing concerns that today's frontier AI models aren't reliable enough for fully autonomous weapons.

This isn't just corporate posturing. With AI increasingly central to defense strategy, the question of where companies draw ethical lines has real consequences for the entire industry. Anthropic's stance may cost them in the short term, but it's setting a precedent that other labs will be measured against.
