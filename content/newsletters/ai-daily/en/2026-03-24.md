## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY: Differentiable Programming

Today's Anthropic science blog introduces a concept worth understanding: **differentiable programming**. The idea is simple but powerful — write your scientific simulation code so that you can automatically compute gradients through it. This means you can optimize parameters by running gradient descent directly through your physics simulation, rather than relying on grid search or MCMC sampling.

Anthropic's example: a cosmological Boltzmann solver (code that predicts the statistical properties of the cosmic microwave background) reimplemented in JAX so that every operation is differentiable. This turns a forward simulation into an optimization machine — you can fit cosmological parameters to observational data orders of magnitude faster.

Why it matters for AI: The same principle underlies neural network training. But applying it to domain-specific scientific code is the next frontier — and it's exactly the kind of task where long-running AI agents excel, because the code is well-scoped and the success criteria (numerical agreement with reference implementations) are clear.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY: Anthropic's Science Blog Launch

The most significant development today isn't a model release — it's Anthropic's triple-post science blog launch. Here's why it matters:

1. **"Vibe physics" is a proof point, not a stunt.** A Harvard professor produced a real physics paper in 2 weeks with Claude. Not a toy demo — a paper with 110 drafts and 36M tokens of iterative refinement. His conclusion: "There is no going back."

2. **The "compressed 21st century" is getting concrete.** Anthropic is explicitly positioning Claude for scientific discovery, not just code generation. The long-running Claude post shows multi-day autonomous workflows for cosmology simulations.

3. **The honest framing matters.** They acknowledge Claude "can hallucinate results, be overly sycophantic, and get stuck on problems a domain practitioner would find trivial." This isn't a sales pitch — it's a field report from the frontier of AI-assisted science.

The implication: if a single professor can do a year's worth of physics in two weeks, the bottleneck in science is shifting from execution to taste and judgment. The researchers who can best direct AI agents will have an enormous productivity advantage.
