## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY: What Are Mini and Nano Models?

When a lab releases a model family like GPT-5.4, it typically comes in several sizes: the full model, a "mini" version, and sometimes a "nano" version. These aren't just smaller — they're specifically optimized for different use cases.

**Full model** (GPT-5.4): Maximum capability, highest cost, best for complex reasoning and frontier tasks. Often has a "Pro" variant that uses even more compute per query.

**Mini** (GPT-5.4 mini): Distilled from the full model to retain most capabilities at 2-3x faster speed and lower cost. Ideal for production workloads where you need good quality at scale — coding, tool use, multimodal understanding.

**Nano** (GPT-5.4 nano): The smallest and cheapest, optimized for simple high-volume tasks — classification, extraction, routing, basic Q&A. Trades some capability for maximum speed and minimum cost.

The key insight: choosing the right size model for each task is one of the most impactful cost-optimization decisions in AI engineering. A well-designed system often uses all three tiers — nano for routing, mini for most work, full/pro for the hardest problems.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY: GPT-5.4 Mini — The New Workhorse

OpenAI's GPT-5.4 mini might be the most practically significant release this week. While frontier models grab headlines, it's the mini-class models that power most real-world AI applications.

What makes 5.4 mini notable: it's 2x faster than GPT-5 mini while bringing 5.4-class capabilities including built-in computer use, tool search (deferring large tool surfaces until runtime), and compaction for long agent sessions. This means production systems can now access capabilities that were frontier-only two weeks ago.

The simultaneous release of nano — optimized purely for speed and cost on simple tasks — signals OpenAI's push to own the full spectrum from frontier reasoning to high-volume commodity tasks. For developers, this means more granular model selection: use nano for routing and classification, mini for the core workload, and full 5.4/Pro only when you need maximum capability.

The combination of subagents (shipped yesterday) + mini/nano (shipped today) suggests OpenAI is building toward a world where a single orchestrator can dynamically dispatch different model tiers to different subtasks — the beginning of true model-aware agent architectures.
