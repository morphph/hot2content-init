## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY: Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)

Today's Qwen 3.5-397B-A17B is a perfect example of **Mixture-of-Experts** architecture. The model has 397 billion total parameters, but only activates 17 billion for any given input — that's the "A17B" (Active 17B).

Think of it like a hospital with 50 specialist doctors, but each patient only sees 2-3 relevant ones. You get the combined knowledge of a massive team while only paying the compute cost of a small one.

MoE models achieve near-dense-model quality at a fraction of the inference cost. The tradeoff: they need more memory to store all experts, even though only a few run at once. This is why GGUF quantization (from projects like llama.cpp) matters — it compresses the idle experts so they fit on consumer hardware.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY: Anthropic's Distillation Accusations

The biggest story today isn't a product launch — it's Anthropic publicly accusing three Chinese AI labs (DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, MiniMax) of "industrial-scale distillation attacks" on Claude models.

**Why this matters:**
- It's the first time a major AI lab has publicly named specific competitors for systematic model theft
- Distillation (training a smaller model to mimic a larger one's outputs) exists in a legal gray zone — it likely violates Terms of Service but may not violate IP law in all jurisdictions
- All three accused labs released new models this week (DeepSeek, Kimi K2.5, MiniMax M2.5), making the timing pointed
- This could reshape the competitive landscape: expect tighter API monitoring, output watermarking, and potential legal action
- It raises fundamental questions about the moat of proprietary AI models in an era where distillation is increasingly effective

Worth watching how this plays out in the coming weeks — it could set precedents for the entire industry.
